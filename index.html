Current Weekly Plan

April 19-24, 2026

Near Wichita, Kansas. Adjust for actual weather, soil moisture, and product labels.

Top Actions

  1. Mow if the lawn is actively growing and tall enough. Keep tall fescue on the taller side, remove no more than one-third of the blade, and favor Sunday through Tuesday before wind and storms.
  2. Check whether crabgrass pre-emergent has already been applied. Use the Kansas spring window if crabgrass has been a real issue. Apply on a calm day and water it in according to the label.
  3. Spot check weeds and thin areas. Spot treat broadleaf weeds only when needed and label conditions are right. Mark thin or bare areas for fall seeding.

Skip This Week

  • Heavy nitrogen fertilizer unless there is a specific reason and summer watering is realistic.
  • Large seeding projects unless there is no alternative; fall is easier for tall fescue.
  • Grub treatment without known history or confirmed signs.

Monitor

  • Redbud bloom and crabgrass timing.
  • Soil moisture after Thursday storms.
  • Brown patches after warm, humid weather.
  • Areas that dry out first.

Annual Lawn Calendar

Tall Fescue Rhythm

January-February

Do: Leave the lawn alone unless debris needs removal. Service the mower and sharpen the blade.

Buy: Mower blade service or soil test materials if needed.

Monitor: Heavy foot traffic on frozen or muddy turf.

Ignore: Winter color.

March

Do: Start mowing when growth begins. Spot treat broadleaf weeds only on a label-approved day.

Buy: Broadleaf spot-treatment product only if weeds are noticeable.

Monitor: Redbud trees and soil warming as crabgrass prevention cues.

Ignore: Heavy early spring fertilizer.

April

Do: Apply crabgrass pre-emergent around redbud full bloom if crabgrass has been a problem.

Buy: Crabgrass pre-emergent if needed.

Monitor: Washout rain after applications and thin areas for fall overseeding.

Ignore: Perfection.

May

Do: Fertilize lightly only if you plan to irrigate through summer or rainfall keeps growth active.

Buy: Slow-release fertilizer or grub prevention only when there is a known reason.

Monitor: Heat, dry spots, mower height, and early disease pressure.

Ignore: Blanket pest treatments.

June-Mid July

Do: Raise the mower to the higher end of the tall fescue range and water only as needed.

Buy: Nothing unless there is a specific problem.

Monitor: Wilting, footprints that do not rebound, gray-blue color, drought stress, and brown patch.

Ignore: Forcing cool-season grass to look lush in Kansas heat.

Late July-August

Do: Keep mowing high, protect the crown, and plan fall seed, aeration, soil testing, and fertilizer.

Buy: Turf-type tall fescue seed and starter fertilizer only if the plan calls for it.

Monitor: Grub damage, heat stress, disease, and bare or thin areas.

Ignore: Major renovation during peak heat.

September

Do: Fertilize around Labor Day. Aerate if compacted. Overseed or seed thin areas.

Buy: Quality tall fescue seed, fall fertilizer, and compost only if it solves a real issue.

Monitor: Germination, watering consistency, washouts, and weeds in new seed.

Ignore: Most weed killers on brand-new grass unless the label allows it.

October

Do: Continue care for new seed. Mow when new grass is ready. Spot treat weeds only if safe for new turf.

Buy: Nothing unless September supplies ran short.

Monitor: Young grass drying out, leaves smothering turf, and broadleaf weeds.

Ignore: Late seeding too close to hard freezes.

November

Do: Apply high-nitrogen fall fertilizer, water it in if needed, and keep leaves from matting.

Buy: Winterizing or high-nitrogen fertilizer.

Monitor: Leaf cover and dry soil before winter.

Ignore: Cosmetic color once cold weather sets in.

December

Do: Clean and store equipment. Review the journal for what worked.

Buy: Nothing unless replacing worn tools.

Monitor: Traffic or debris damaging turf.

Ignore: Lawn anxiety. The year is basically done.
